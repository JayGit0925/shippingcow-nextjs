If your shipping bill has climbed every year since 2020 — even though your order volume stayed flat — you're not imagining things. Parcel carrier revenue per package has compounded steadily upward, and most sellers assume it's unavoidable.

It isn't. But first you have to understand what's actually driving it.

There are five mechanisms inflating your shipping invoice. Most sellers only know about one of them.

---

## Cost Driver 1: Annual GRI (General Rate Increases)

UPS and FedEx announce General Rate Increases each January. The stated percentage is usually 6–9%. That sounds manageable — until you compound it.

Since 2020, cumulative GRI has added more than 40% to base parcel rates. A shipment that cost $28 in 2020 now costs roughly $40 before any surcharges are applied.

The increase in your invoice year-over-year is not random. It is a scheduled, predictable extraction.

---

## Cost Driver 2: Fuel Surcharges

FedEx currently charges a **27% fuel surcharge** on every shipment.

That percentage is applied to your base shipping charge — not to the fuel cost itself. So a $40 shipment carries $10.80 in fuel surcharge. For a seller shipping 300 heavy parcels per month, that's $3,240/month in fuel surcharge alone.

Fuel surcharges are recalculated weekly using a formula tied to the U.S. Energy Information Administration's average retail fuel price index. When fuel prices drop, surcharges come down slowly. When fuel prices rise, they adjust fast. They haven't been in single digits since 2021.

The practical impact: the fuel surcharge is a second rate increase layered on top of GRI, reset weekly in the carrier's favor.

---

## Cost Driver 3: DIM Weight Billing

Dimensional weight (DIM weight) is the mechanism by which carriers charge you based on the *volume* of a package, not its actual weight — when the volume-derived weight exceeds the actual weight.

The formula: **Length × Width × Height ÷ DIM Divisor = DIM Weight**

UPS and FedEx use a divisor of **139** for domestic shipments. Consider what that means for a slightly oversized box:

A 26×20×14" box has a volume of 7,280 cubic inches. Divide by 139: **DIM weight = 52.4 lbs**. If your actual product weighs 35 lbs, you pay for 52.4 lbs — 50% more than the product weighs.

Add two inches to each dimension (28×22×16") and volume jumps to 9,856 in³. DIM weight at 139: **70.9 lbs**. You're now paying for 71 lbs on a 35-lb product.

DIM weight is the lever with the most seller control. The DIM divisor your carrier uses is not a law of physics — it's a business decision. Different parties use different numbers.

---

## Cost Driver 4: Zone Hell

Every per-pound charge is multiplied by the shipping zone — a number from 2 to 8 that represents the distance between your origin ZIP and the destination ZIP.

Zone 2 to Zone 8 on the same package can mean a **2.5× difference** in cost. A 65-lb package that ships for $29 at Zone 2 can cost $72 at Zone 8.

Most sellers ship from one location. If your warehouse is in New Jersey, every customer west of the Mississippi is Zone 6–8. If 40% of your orders go to California, Texas, and Arizona, you're absorbing maximum-zone pricing on 40% of your volume, every day.

Zone exposure is structural. The only real solution is distributing inventory closer to your customers.

---

## Cost Driver 5: Accessorial Surcharges

Residential delivery fee, address correction fee, Saturday delivery surcharge, large package surcharge — these line items appear on carrier invoices under generic names and compound silently.

For heavy-goods sellers, the most common accumulation:

- **Residential delivery:** ~$5.55/package at FedEx (most DTC orders go to homes)
- **Large package surcharge:** triggered when a package's length plus girth exceeds 165 inches
- **Address correction:** $17.50+ if the carrier has to look up the correct address

On a typical 65-lb furniture parcel, accessorial charges can add $8–18 per shipment on top of everything else.
